# CBRE Tone of Voice

The operational distillation of CBRE's official **Tone of Voice Guidelines (2021)**. Source PDF: `C:\Users\TBaaij\OneDrive - CBRE, Inc\Documents\cbre_tov_guidelines.pdf` (full text mirrored in `references/source-guidelines.md`). For brand assets, templates, fonts: `brand.cbre.com`; questions: `CBREbrand@cbre.com`.

## How to use this

- **Standalone:** invoke it when writing or reviewing any CBRE-facing copy. Apply the seven writing principles to every sentence; reach into the *Attributes in Action* menu only where an approach fits naturally — never force one into every line.
- **As a shared reference:** other CBRE skills read this file **while drafting** (content-lock stage), so the voice shapes the words as they're written. Voice is *infused*, not *appended* — it should never make content longer, and a "run it through afterward" pass is the wrong model. The one legitimate after-pass is a mechanical compliance lint (banned words, passive voice, overclaiming) — the checklist at the bottom.

## North star

CBRE creates the real estate solutions of tomorrow, so businesses and people thrive. Every piece of copy ties, somewhere, back to that — we describe what we do *and* connect it to a larger story about seeing potential and realizing opportunity. The brand vision ("Realizing Potential in Every Dimension") inspires the writing; it is never used as a tagline or pasted verbatim.

## The three attributes

We write for a blend of all three at once — clarity kept interesting by a strong point of view, boldness kept honest by being about the client, connection kept credible by staying professional.

| | **Clear** | **Bold** | **Connected** |
|---|---|---|---|
| In one line | Straightforward and direct about what we do | We take a stand and deliver forward-looking guidance | Unified globally by markets, relationships, and services |
| **DO** | Convey ideas simply; use commonly understood language | Be declarative; share a strong point of view when we have one | Be conversational; bring in out-of-category ideas; credit partners and clients (with permission) |
| **DON'T** | Use jargon; take clarity to a bland place — we are interesting and opinionated | Be argumentative; overclaim; be arrogant — it's the vision we share *with* the client, not what we know alone | Get too informal — we stay in a professional context |

How an audience should feel reading us: a **sophisticated expert who is also approachable** — a knowledgeable leader you turn to for the big questions, dynamic and opinionated, but who knows what's relevant and when to keep it simple.

## The seven writing principles — the operational core

Apply these to **everything**, not just headlines (voice lives in body copy and footnotes too).

1. **Have a point of view.** Step up and say what we think. State opinions clearly; avoid equivocation and vague hedging.
2. **Say it simply.** Choose plain words over jargon and business-speak; cut unnecessary adjectives. Our audience is global, many reading in a second language — clarity is the priority.
3. **Less is more.** Saying too much overwhelms. Weigh every word; focus on what matters most.
4. **Convey action.** Active voice, not passive. Pick verbs that show CBRE *doing* — "We created a solution," not "A solution was developed."
5. **Don't be punny.** Clever real-estate puns are an industry trope and make us blend in. Skip them.
6. **Be conversational, but professional.** Write like a real person; it's fine to use conjunctions and a sentence fragment can be a great headline. But don't trade away credibility — "Let's talk" works; "Hey, what's up??" doesn't.
7. **Don't be egotistical.** Balance bold claims about our capability with the benefit to others — "We provide unmatched intelligence *so that* our clients can make the right decisions."

## Volume control — dial up or down by deliverable

Same personality, adjusted to the room. This is the single most important calibration when writing across CBRE deliverables.

| Dial **DOWN** (subdued, clarity-first) | Dial **UP** (fullest expression) |
|---|---|
| Investor relations, earnings, board/advisory decks, IR, press releases, capital-strategy memos | Thought leadership, market overviews, advertising, social, web |
| Lead with the facts, simply stated. Keep a POV and active voice, but restrain intensifiers and proclamatory headlines. *"Q4 investment activity surged 97% from Q3; annual volume down 34% from 2019."* | Proclamations, maxims, intensified word choice, open questions all welcome. *"The era of resilience is here."* |

Business development sits between — match the client and the opportunity. **How to adjust:** (1) follow the attributes and principles as much as possible, (2) select only the approaches that fit the context, (3) look for what works. Even a dialed-down piece still carries the voice — it's never absent, just quieter.

## Attributes in Action — the approach menu

A library to *dial up* an attribute where it fits. Use sparingly and only where natural; there's no need to use one in every sentence.

**Clear**
- **Identify the steps** — impose order by breaking something into clear components/steps.
- **Label a phenomenon** — coin a term for something new ("the efficiency-happiness gap"). (For an existing thing, use the established category language.)
- **Define what it's not** — clarify by contrast ("Big ideas aren't enough. You must put them into action.").
- **Let the numbers speak** — lead with a striking stat, let the reader draw the conclusion, then add the payoff ("56% of US office workers want flexible work benefits. That's a major cultural shift.").

**Bold**
- **Make a proclamation** — declare what we see from our vantage point ("The data revolution has forever changed real estate.").
- **Create a maxim** — a fundamental truth, succinctly ("If you only see part of the picture, you're working in the dark.").
- **Intensify your word choice** — heighten one or two words for a strong image ("a metamorphosis to our cities"). Don't overdo it; don't pile on adjectives.
- **Issue a challenge** — call the audience to act ("The world needs green buildings and inclusive workplaces. We must all do more.").

**Connected**
- **Say what they're thinking** — name the audience's concern or hope directly.
- **Identify a linkage** — connect an insight from one area to another to show our breadth ("What can data centers learn from big oil?").
- **Acknowledge others** — credit a partner or client to show (and strengthen) connection. Get permission before referencing client projects.
- **Ask an open-ended question** — open a door, invite a response ("How is climate change affecting your real estate choices?").

## Before / after — the shape of a fix

> **Before (formal, internal-feeling):** "Procurement and supply chain management have become some of the most relevant topics in corporate responsibility… With an annual global spend of more than $50 billion… CBRE recognizes the opportunity to influence suppliers…"
>
> **After (bold, clear, connected):** "Procurement and supply chain management are the next frontier in corporate responsibility. Purchasing power must be used to encourage positive change. Because CBRE handles $50 billion in contracts each year… we're able to get a complete view of an industry-wide supply chain… Want to learn how we do it?"

What changed: a proclamation headline; active voice; simpler and shorter; an opinion stated plainly; bold action without ego; an open question to connect.

## Fast compliance checklist (the legitimate after-pass)

Run these as a final lint on finished copy:

- [ ] **Active voice** throughout — no "was developed / is being managed / has been delivered" where CBRE is the actor.
- [ ] **No jargon / business-speak** that a second-language reader would stumble on.
- [ ] **No puns** in headlines or copy.
- [ ] **Has a point of view** — no vague, hedged, "it could be argued" filler.
- [ ] **Not egotistical** — every capability claim is paired with the client benefit.
- [ ] **No overclaiming** — bold but defensible.
- [ ] **Volume matches the deliverable** (see the table above).
- [ ] **Avoid model-translated words:** prefer "answer / result / finding" over "verdict"; avoid overusing "thesis," "leverage," "robust," "holistic."
- [ ] **Ties to the brand story** somewhere — we don't just describe, we connect to realizing potential.
